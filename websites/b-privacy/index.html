B‑Privacy

Defining and Enforcing Privacy in Weighted Voting

Abstract

In traditional one-vote-per-person systems, privacy means ballot secrecy: tallies are public, but individual choices stay hidden. Weighted-voting systems, now common in cryptocurrency and web3, break this assumption. We show that publishing raw tallies often reveals how individuals voted, even with secret ballots.

Weighted voting therefore needs a new privacy framework. We introduce B-privacy, which measures the economic cost for an adversary to bribe voters based on revealed tallies, and propose a mechanism that boosts B-privacy by noising tallies, with provable security bounds on the tradeoff against transparency (tally accuracy).

Analyzing 3,582 proposals across 30 DAOs, we find that large voters ("whales") fundamentally limit any mechanism aimed at increasing B-privacy. Still, for proposals with lower weight concentration, our mechanism raises B-privacy by a geometric mean factor of 4.1× with low transparency loss, offering the first principled guidance on transparency-privacy tradeoffs in weighted voting.

Noise as a defense

A straightforward solution to those attacks is to publish a noised tally instead of the raw count: a small amount of calibrated randomness is added to each option’s total. While if done correctly the outcome stays the same, the noise shifts the result so the attacker can no longer be sure which weight went where with 100% confidence.

We ran the same deanonymization attack on noised tallies, and found that noise can substantially reduce the fraction of ballots and voting weight an attacker can recover.

Privacy as bribery cost

The whale and precision attacks show that tallies can leak, but they are only heuristics. They don't tell us what the strongest adversary can do against a given disclosure rule. For that, we need a well-defined privacy notion. Existing definitions in the token-weighted setting diverge sharply depending on whether they focus on leaked weights or leaked ballots, so we propose a definition that instead captures leakage through its impact on the election: the more a tally reveals, the cheaper it becomes for an adversary to bribe voters into flipping the outcome.

B‑privacy is the minimum total bribe an adversary must commit to flip an outcome with high confidence, given only what the published tally reveals. Bribes are enforceable only when the adversary can verify compliance from the tally, so the disclosure rule directly sets the price of influence.

Case study: B‑Privacy in Aavegotchi

To see how B‑privacy behaves on real governance data, we analyse 250 proposals from the Aavegotchi DAO and measure how relative B‑privacy responds to tally perturbation. We group proposals by their Minimum Decisive Coalition (MDC) — the smallest set of voters whose combined weight could have changed the outcome — because proposals with larger MDCs are inherently harder to corrupt.

Even modest noise pays off: across proposals with MDC ≥ 5, the geometric mean of relative B‑privacy improves by ×4.1. For elections with less concentrated voting power, noise can substantially increase the cost of bribery with ×1000 from just a 2% perturbation in proposals with MDC ≥ 30.

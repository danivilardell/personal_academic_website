Abstract

Coercion-resistance (CR) is the gold-standard property of secure voting: voters can cast ballots reflecting their true preferences even under active interaction with an adversary. Existing CR voting schemes face a fundamental tradeoff: fake-credential schemes need untappable channels for registration making them impractical, while deniable revoting schemes only achieve a weaker CR notion that is not sufficient in online voting.

We close this gap with Cast Iron, the first publicly verifiable fake-credential voting system without untappable channels. At its heart is a new primitive, the Covert Timestamped Commitment (CTC), which lets voters self-register privately by covertly hiding the registration inside public traffic; with no registration authority, and at any time, even years before an election takes place.

We prove security in the UC framework, implement a prototype that verifiably tallies 1 million ballots in ~1,350 core-hours (under $100 on commodity cloud), and introduce CoCo, a unifying economic metric of CR. Under CoCo, Cast Iron achieves a 25× cost-of-coercion advantage over comparable revoting protocols in the fully online setting.

The challenge of online voting

Online voting makes coercion greatly scalable. Smart-contract platforms have spawned vote-buying marketplaces worth millions; ballot secrecy alone does nothing to stop them, since a voter can provably decrypt their ballot for a briber, showing compliance in order to get the payout. Coercion-resistance instead aims to give voters a way to evade adversarial pressure even while interacting with the adversary. Coercion-resistance protects against such attacks. Since its introduction in 2005, two design philosophies to achieve this property have emerged, and each with its own tradeoffs.

Approach A

Fake credentials

When coerced, the voter hands over a credential that looks real but is silently invalid. The briber has no way to tell.

Catch: the voter must register their real credential through an untappable channel — a channel that produces no transcript at all. Practical only in person, or with special-purpose hardware.

Approach B

Deniable revoting

If coerced into casting a ballot, the voter quietly casts another later. The latest ballot wins.

Catch: if the adversary is online at the close of the election, they can simply race the voter to cast last. Recent work [Loki] tried to fix this and was promptly broken by brute-force and statistical attacks.

Cast Iron sits squarely in the gap. It is a fake-credential scheme, but it does not require an untappable channel. The trick is to look closely at what the untappable channel was actually buying us.

How to secure a registration

Fake-credential CR fundamentally requires that the adversary cannot distinguish a voter who registered a credential from one who did not. Untappable channels achieve this by hiding the registration transcript entirely. But the same indistinguishability can be achieved a different way:

Even with cooperation between the adversary and the voter, given the publicly available transcript, the adversary should not be able to tell whether a voter has registered or not.

An untappable channel trivially achieves that, as it does not leak any information about the communiation between the voter and the registration authority. Such untappable channel however is not reasonable in online elections, as registration will always leak at least the network traffic. Cast Iron realizes this through covert self-registration. There is no registrar. Voters generate their own voting credentials and post them anonymously to a public blockchain — in a form indistinguishable from ordinary transactions already flowing through the chain. The adversary sees the same transactions everyone else sees, and has no way to tell which one (if any) is a registration.

Covert Timestamped Commitments

The primitive that powers Cast Iron is a Covert Timestamped Commitment (CTC): a way to commit to a value at a verifiable time without leaving any sign that you committed. Our construction hides the commitment inside the ECDSA signature nonce of an otherwise ordinary blockchain transaction. The nonce is uniformly random under the elliptic-curve discrete log, so a hash-based commitment slots in perfectly — the resulting transaction is computationally indistinguishable from background traffic.

Live blockchain feed balance-emptying transfers, recent blocks

Every transaction here looks the same: a sender, a recipient, a value, and an ECDSA signature whose nonce is a random-looking number. Most are normal transfers.

One of these is a CTC. The voter has chosen the signature nonce rσ to be a hash-based commitment H(m ‖ r) — where m is their secret voting credential.

Open. The voter reveals (m, r) to a verifier, who recomputes the commitment and confirms the on-chain signature was produced with that exact nonce. The block height pins down when the commitment was made.

Key insight. From the outside, every transaction looks alike. The adversary observes the chain and gains no information about who, if anyone, registered a credential.

On Tron, ~5 such balance-emptying transactions arrive per block. A 1-minute window therefore yields a registration anonymity set of ~100, translating to a 25× cost-of-coercion advantage. A full day yields ~144,000, pushing the advantage past 900×.

An election with no registration window

Removing the registration authority does more than simplify deployment: it removes the registration window entirely. In existing fake-credential schemes the voter must register inside a fixed window, before voting opens. The adversary therefore has a fixed moment they can race to. In Cast Iron, the only constraint on registration is that it happen after the voter's inalienable credential exists — potentially years before an election is even announced.

Existing fake-credential schemes registration window with a registrar
Cast Iron register anytime, no authority

Both timelines start the same way: the voter's inalienable credential is created at t₀, and the election ends with tallying.

In existing schemes, the voter must interact with a registrar R during a prescribed window [tR, t₁]. The adversary knows the window and can station themselves at its start.

In Cast Iron, registration is a single self-issued post to the bulletin board — possible at any time after t₀. The voter may have registered before any coercion attempt, or any election announcement.

Why it matters. The unbounded window means the adversary can never identify a moment at which voters are guaranteed to be unregistered. No matter how quickly they act, the voter could already have registered — whether or not they actually did.

Cost of Coercion (CoCo)

Different CR schemes have historically used incompatible security definitions, making cross-protocol comparison difficult. We introduce CoCo: a protocol-agnostic, game-theoretic metric. CoCo measures the economic cost to an adversary of swaying an election with target probability p, normalized by the same cost under a non-coercion-resistant baseline.

CoCo of k means an adversary must spend k× as much to sway a Cast Iron election as they would to sway an equivalent unprotected one. Higher is better.

In the fully online setting, deniable revoting collapses to CoCo ≈ 1 — no harder to attack than no CR at all — because rational voters who expect to defect successfully half the time accept correspondingly smaller bribes. Cast Iron achieves a flat 25× advantage across all target win probabilities.
